Learn the basics

Pricing, explained in plain English

No jargon. Just what each term means and why it matters to your bottom line.

What is cost price?

The amount you pay to buy or produce one unit — the product itself, plus freight, packaging or direct labor. Everything else is built on top of this number.

What is selling price?

The amount your customer pays. It has to cover your cost, your target profit, and often VAT — get it wrong and every sale quietly works against you.

What is profit?

Selling price minus cost price. Simple in theory — but only accurate if you know both numbers correctly and haven't confused markup with margin along the way.

What is markup?

Profit shown as a percentage of your cost price. A 100 OMR item sold for 125 OMR has a 25% markup — the extra 25 OMR is a quarter of what you paid.

What is gross profit margin?

Profit shown as a percentage of your selling price instead. That same 25 OMR profit on a 125 OMR selling price is only a 20% margin, because it's measured against the bigger number.

Why they're never equal

Markup divides profit by cost. Margin divides the same profit by selling price, which is always the larger figure. A bigger denominator always gives a smaller percentage, so margin is always below markup.

Common pricing mistakes

Applying a margin target as if it were a markup, forgetting VAT until after prices are printed, and copying a competitor's shelf price without knowing their cost base.

How businesses quietly lose money

A shortfall of just a few currency units per unit looks harmless on one sale. Multiply it by daily volume and a full year, and it becomes a serious hit to profit — see the impact calculator above.

A real-world example

A supermarket wants a 20% margin on a 100 OMR case of stock. Priced at 120 OMR it only reaches 16.67%. Priced at 125 OMR it hits the real target — try both in the calculator above.

Markup is the amount you add on top of your cost price, shown as a percentage of that cost. If a product costs 100 OMR and you sell it for 125 OMR, your markup is 25%, because the 25 OMR profit is 25% of the 100 OMR cost.

Gross profit margin is the percentage of your selling price that is profit. Using the same example, 25 OMR profit on a 125 OMR selling price is a 20% margin, because the profit is measured against the selling price, not the cost.

Multiply the cost price by 1 plus the markup percentage. For a 100 OMR cost and a 25% markup, the selling price is 100 × 1.25 = 125 OMR.

Divide the cost price by 1 minus the margin percentage. For a 100 OMR cost and a 20% target margin, the selling price is 100 ÷ (1 − 0.20) = 125 OMR.
